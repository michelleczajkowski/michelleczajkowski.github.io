Observing the Academic Writing Process and Product

Task and Task Development

The project, as a whole, asks how we can best help those first year students who lack adequate academic writing skills by means of diagnostic testing. By adquate academic writing skills we mean: The writing skills and language knowledge that, while still in need of development, are sufficiently developed so that any deficiencies that exist will not negatively affect study success.

In the study, we ask firstly:

What are the features of academic writing as needed for a well written undergraduate paper?

From this we ask:

For each of these features, what characterises 'sufficiently developed' performance?

The first of these questions defines the construct we wish to measure. The second probes a 'cut point' for each feature that makes up the construct.

The answer to the first question will be drawn from the literature, considering other taxonomies and categorization of features, and whether these were intuitively constructed by experts or empirically explored through research. The issue of general academic writing vs faculty- or program-specific writing should also be considered, as well as the level of writing we can expect at the first year of undergraduate work vs the last year vs graduate writing. From this foundation, a task can be developed to elicit what we wish to capture and observe.

The second question should be answered empirically, drawing on performance from the target group. Because this study focuses on Radboud University, and its post-entry diagnostic assessment taken by all first-year students, data should be collected from this popuation. Therefore, we will collect evidence of the writing skills of Radboud undergraduates in their first year, the task output.

This section details task development by breaking down question 1 above into these sub-questions:

- What kind of writing do (first-year) undergraduates do?

- What are the (observable) features of this type of writing (in product and process)?

- What kind of writing task will elicit these features?

What kind of writing do (first-year) undergraduates do?

Type of writing and writing conditions  As detailed in Lunsford and Lunsford (2008), the type of writing expected at undergraduate level has become increasingly complex and demanding. Their survey found that  undergraduate assignment length was increasing from an average of 162 words in 1917, to 422 in 1986 to 1038 in 2006. Additionally, while students from previous generations were asked to mainly write personal narratives, the majority of writing assignments in 2006 was found to be either researched reports or arguments with very few or no sources. Research and argumentation are therefore likely far more relevant than other types of writing for our purposes.

Additionally, while many courses include some essay-type exams which are taken under carefully proctored conditions, academic writing itself generally is not typified by this environment. Rather, students are given an assignment and a due date, and decide where to write, how to write, and how long to write for themselves.

The task for this study will ask participants to write a short academic text which requires a degree of argumentation and should follow the coventional style for research. It will not be written in an unfamiliar environment but rather, to the extent possible, at a place (likely their home) and time of their choosing. There will be a time limit by necessity, but it will be extremely generous; double the expected time. Participants will be told that e.g., 45 minutes is a reasonable goal, but you can take up to 1.5 hours.

General academic or faculty-specific?    Different academic programs in different faculties require different types of writing. This is particularly salient when it comes to topic, but also applies to genre, writing purpose, length / brevity of typical writing, and similar considerations. Given this, it is important to consider whether this study, in the interest of authenticity and recognition of these differences, should offer different tasks for participants from different faculties. Additionally, the diagnostic language assessment (RADAr) itself offers faculty specific test versions; as this study will serve to help validate the RADAr, there is an argument to similarly offer faculty-specific tasks.

This was considered but ultimately rejected for a number of reasons. Firstly, the data collection process will be time-consuming, with an average of two hours for each partcipants. Because of this, we do not expect to have a group of participants above 40 or 50. If we were to use five or six different tasks, this would need accounting for in the statistical analyses, and would potentially weaken conclusions drawn from the data. To keep the results robust for a small (by necessity) group, non-essential factors should be reduced or eliminated.

Additionally, while different programs and faculties require different kinds and amounts of writing, this kind of genre and domain awareness develops over the course of a degree. In the first year, students are still navigating the bridge between secondary and tertiary education; as such, students are generally not expected to possess such specialized knowledge yet. Rather, students at this stage will be more familiar with a more general type of academic writing, as this is more typical of writing instruction and assessment. The choice for a general task for all for this study therefore seems a suitable strategy to elicit the expected writing skills of first-year students.

Finally, the RADAr, the diagnostic test at the heart of this study, produces faculty-specific versions. It is therefore clearly a distinction that the test developers, and by extention the university as a whole, feel is important to some extent. However, analysis and validation of the test shows that these faculty differences are largely superficial and done mainly to increase face validity and, consequently, uptake of feedback, rather than to measure faculty-specific skills (though it undoubably does this to some small degree as a matter of course). Given also that the skills and knowledge measured in the RADAr is designed to be uniform across all faculty versions, the same skills and knowledge can be captured through a general for-all task.

Characteristics of academic writing         Our task will focus on argumentation in a research context, and will be written with access to resources, no time limit, and in a familiar environment. As such, we hope to mimic writing as it is authentically done for these students. In developing a task, we must first consider the features that make up academic writing in this context; we can then ensure our task provides opportunities for writers to adequately display their level of mastery over each.

Characteristic of the academic writing product    One way of compiling such a list would be to look at how instructors respond to undergraduate student writing via their written feedback. Lunsford and Lunsford (2008) again offer some guidance; their study coded instructor feedback, and found that  instructors tend to focus on discrete, easy to identify and correct errors like spelling, incorrect grammar, etc. However, this is not a consistent finding. Bridgeman and Carlson (1984, survey of academic writing) found that instructors reported focusing on discourse-level characteristics rather than word or sentence level. These results are not necesarily incongruent. Instructors with large classes report finding it difficult to give extensive personalized feedback, particularly when it may be counterproductive (Ackerman & Gross, 2010, Instructor Feedback) and while many may evaluate discourse-level characteristics, they may opt to focus on surface level features in their feedback. All in all, it is difficult to gain a clear picture good academic writing as instructor feedback is influenced by many factors other than text quality.  

A robust supplemental stream of evidence on the type of writing (and skills) needed at the first-year undergraduate level can be found via a composition or writing instruction and assessment. Research done on large-scale tests like the TOEFL, which are designed to evaluate a students academic writing ability in the context of university admissions, offer evidence relating to domain definition, or how well test tasks reflect performance on the target langauge use domain. Knoch et al. (2014) reviewed the literature on what general writing ability, academic writing ability and general language proficiency entail, and synthesized this into a list of eight features: accuracy, fluency, (syntactic and lexical) complexity, coherence, cohesion, content, orientation to source evidence, and metadiscourse. While this list was by necessity limited to those features that could be identified and coded in the writing product, it may not be complete for our purposes, which will include the writing process itself. However, this can serve as a good starting point. Knoch et al. also discusses at length how each of these features could be measured, and so may serve as a guide for the analysis of this study's data.

Knoch et al.'s list reflects, or at least does not contradict, a focus on argumentation and research, identified by Lunsford and Lunsford (2008) as the most commonly required type of writing in undergraduate study. Nearly all the features of the 8-feature list would be observable in most kinds of writing, including argumentative and research writing, though the complexity of the task will affect performance on each. Three deserve further mention here: content, orientation to source evidence, and metadiscourse, as these are rarer to find in writing assessment rubrics, are not as well researched, and do not have as many established methods to analyze them.

Content refers roughly to the density of information. There are no standard ways of objectively measuring content. In many rating rubrics, content appears as part of 'task achievement' or similar. However, what amount of content is 'sufficient' (and therefore deserving of a higher score) is subjective. Knoch et al. coded ideas and classified them as main or supporting ideas.

Orientation to Source Evidence includes voice and use of input material. The former indicates the source of information (the writer, another study, an accepted truth). The later involves appropriate paraphrasing and integrating source material, and in the case of research papers, citing them appropriately.

Metadiscourse refers to the way the writer recognizes, connects with and assists the reader. It is language that marks the direction and purpose of the text.

While accuracy, fluency, complexity, and to some extent coherence and cohesion are easy to classify along a continuum from 'poor' to 'good', features like number of ideas, use of voice, citations, and metadiscourse are less clearly linear. The non-linear development of some of these features, vis a vis writing rating scales, is a recognized issue in assessments. However, for the purposes of diagnostic testing, and our study, no such linear scale for each feature is needed. It is only necessary that the task itself facilitates elicitation of these features.

                Characteristic of the academic writing product    Proficiency tests like TOEFL take as the basis for evaluation the writing product alone. Some elements of the writing process are reflected in task constraints; word count minimums and time limits reflect elements of the writing process (e.g. fluency). However, such tests are mainly concerned with the text itself; how to improve the product by examining and changing the process is, for these tests, something that is limited to test preparation. Therefore, the most direct access to the features of the writing process we wish to be able to observe may be to look at test preparation materials.
