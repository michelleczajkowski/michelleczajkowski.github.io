Introduction

Writing is usually assessed by examining a written text. However, problems that are visible in the final written product are sometimes only symptoms of something going on in the writing process. Think, for example, on surface-level errors in mechanics. Are they due to inattention? Running out of time? The writer's inability to 'see' errors? Or gaps in the writer's language knowledge? Only the writing process can reveal the answer*.

It is increasingly easy to capture the writing process; the technology to do so is increasingly accessible. In many writing tests, we already record the writing process as part of computer-based proctoring systems. We are reaching a point where language assessors and instructors will be able to access both the product and the process of writing fairly easily.

In diganostic assessment contexts, this could be very helpful. Rating rubrics, for example, could include criteria related to planning, time management, and revision efficiency. Teachers and course designers could also gain a better idea of what students are struggling with. As much of writing didactics focus on how to write, this would be useful information.

The Study

This website introduces a proposed study to explore how the writing process can be evaluated and assessed in formative or diagnostic contexts. In Step 2, it provides an example of student writing, and then in Step 3, a video (8x speed) of the text being written. Many teachers and testers may never have seen such a video in full, and find it insightful.
